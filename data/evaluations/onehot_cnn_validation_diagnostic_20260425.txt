Key finding:
  CNN memorizes the training split much faster and much more completely than MLP.
  However, CNN validation policy accuracy remains around 4-6%, essentially the same as MLP and close to random among many legal moves.
  CNN validation policy loss gets dramatically worse as training proceeds, rising from 3.5455 at epoch 10 to 11.4858 at epoch 80.

Interpretation:
  The issue is not simply that MLP lacks spatial inductive bias.
  With one-hot MCTS targets, both MLP and CNN fail to generalize policy top-1 to held-out positions.
  CNN proves the labels can be memorized, but the learned policy does not transfer.
  This strongly suggests that one-hot top-action imitation from noisy/flat teacher visit distributions is a poor supervised target for generalization.

Conclusion:
  Do not switch directly to CNN deployment based on this result.
  Do not continue one-hot supervised training as the main improvement route.
  The bottleneck is likely target quality / target formulation rather than only model capacity.

Recommended next steps:
  1. Return to soft policy training or moderately sharpened policy_temperature=0.25, but evaluate with validation KL/cross-entropy rather than one-hot top-1 alone.
  2. Generate stronger/lower-noise teacher targets: more simulations, lower self-play action temperature, or store deterministic teacher best moves only for high-confidence positions.
  3. Filter or weight samples by target policy confidence/entropy, because previous diagnostics showed only a small fraction of positions have a clear best action.
  4. Consider game-level split for validation to avoid leakage analysis ambiguity, though random split already shows no useful policy generalization.
